St. Augustine once said, "What then is time? If no one asks me, I know what it is. If I wish to explain it to him who asks, I do not know".

Aristotle viewed the present as something continually changing, the Roman emperor-philosopher Marcus Aurelius described time as a river of passing events. And in the West, at least, many would still identify with these ideas. Much time feels like something that flows in one direction, British physicist Arthur Eddington called this, “the Arrow of Time".

Physicists define time as the progression of events from the past to the present into the future. Time is considered to be the fourth dimension of reality; it is used to describe events in three-dimensional space. It is not something we can see, touch, or taste; nor it is something we can send, borrow or save, we can only measure its passage.

In 1907, a German mathematician and professor Hermann Minkowski, showed that the special theory of relativity of 1905, developed by his former student Albert Einstein, could be understood geometrically as a theory of four-dimensional space-time, now known as the "Minkowski spacetime". He once said, “Henceforth space by itself, and time by itself, are doomed to fade away into mere shadows, and only a kind of union of the two will preserve an independent reality". In essence: Matter creates time. In the absence of matter, time is irrelevant.

Although some physicists propose that time does not exist, time perception (our sense of time) does. This is why the evidence from physics is at odds with how life feels. Our shared idea of what the concept of “future” or “past” means may not apply to everything everywhere in the universe, but it does reflect the reality of our lives here on earth. Isaac Newton wrote in his masterwork, the Principia, “Absolute, true, and mathematical time, in and of itself and of its own nature, flows uniformly”.

In spite of universal acceptance of the Newtonian concept of absolute time, Albert Einstein deduced that there is no fixed frame of reference in the universe. He showed in his theory of special relativity that time is relative. He demonstrated that time moves slowly if an object is moving fast. An object in motion experiences time dilation, meaning that time moves more slowly when one is moving, than when one is standing still. "Einstein concluded that simultaneity is relative; events that are simultaneous for one observer may not be for another," the Encyclopedia Britannica stated. "This led him to the counterintuitive idea that time flows differently according to the state of motion, and to the conclusion that distance is also relative."

Before we go any further into the different perspectives of the age of the universe, let’s refresh our understanding and knowledge of cosmology.

In 1929, astronomer Edwin Hubble (1889-1953) discovered that distance galaxies were moving away from the earth due to the observed light's wavelength was stretched towards the red spectrum (i.e. redshift). The discovery led to the conclusion that, at some point in time, the universe must have started with a small beginning, and consequently gave rise to the support of the “Big Bang” theory of the universe first proposed by Georges Lemaître in 1927. Subsequent observations of the CMB, the residual heat of creation - faint afterglow of light with nearly uniform intensity that fills the universe, and measurements by the WMAP in 2001 placed a date of the “Big Bang” to approximately 13.77 billion years ago.

At the moment of the "Big Bang", this very dense and extremely hot speck of space undergone a process of exponential expansion or stretching called “inflation”. This rapid inflation produced a hot radiant of energy which would allow matters to form as described by Einstein's equation E = mc2, or m = E/c2 (i.e. Mass of matter m equals to Energy E divided by the square of the velocity of light c). The universe cooled as it continued to expand and created a condition giving rise to the building block of particles/anti-particles and later matters (i.e. quarks, photons and electrons). As a result, protons and neutrons were formed at their respective threshold temperatures. When matter was created, the cosmic clock started ticking. However, the relative passage of time varies with the creation of matters (i.e. stars and galaxies due to their differences in local gravity and velocities). The universe has since cooled to its current temperature of about 2.73 oK (0o Kelvin K = - 273.15 oC).

2. History Timeline

Einstein wrote to the widow of his friend Besso, "for us believing physicists, the distinction between past, present and future is only an illusion, even if a stubborn one".

Human history starts with the first humans, Adam and Eve, and which is believed, according to the Jewish tradition, to be 3761 B.C. For nearly 6,000 years, human activities (conflicts and trade) and the environment (famine and natural disaster) caused a major shift in the way plant and animal communities were structured. Along with animal domestication, human civilization enters a period dominated by agricultural farming, herding, and ranching. We move from being primarily hunter-gatherers to an early agrarian culture. Alongside our cultural advances, human culture and knowledge developed at a spectacular rate. Empires rise and fall across the globe, as do various religions. Scientific advances begin to occur, transforming our understanding of the universe.

The timeline begins with Adam, as the Hebrew year count starts in year 3761 B.C. Years in the calendar are designated AM to identify them as part of the Anno Mundi epoch, indicating the age of the world according to the interpretation of the Bible. The Hebrew calendar is comparatively imprecise in terms of reflecting the duration of a solar year, which is the time it takes the earth to complete a full orbit around the sun.

A day may be added to or subtracted from specific months in the calendar to prevent certain holidays (such as Rosh Hashana) from falling on certain days of the week. A leap year in the Hebrew calendar has 13 months and it is periodically added to approximately stay in sync with the astronomical seasons and it occurs seven times in a 19-year Metonic cycle. This way, the Hebrew calendar deviates from the solar year by one day every 216 years.

Events occur every day all over the world, one part may be celebrating an achievement, other part may be recovering from a disaster, and some are just a footnote in a history book. The years in which events happened long ago may not coincide with events happened in other parts of the world as different calendars were utilized to record events. Until civilization adopts a common or international system of recording the passage of time, comparing the record of events in different parts of the world seems futile. Nevertheless, attempts have been made to reconcile the differences and established some common understanding of the discrepancies between different ways of recording the passage of time.

This History Timeline includes ancient civilizations using different systems for time keeping, therefore some of the events may be only an approximation.
